TOMMI APP

Context: Microsoft New Technologist Intern Program


Duration: 7 weeks (Summer 2019)


Collaboration: Member of 5 person team


Tools: Adobe Illustrator, Figma, Xcode IDE, Swift, Git

As a team member I designed and engineered an iOS app Tommi, a lovable, interactive avatar that helps parents balance their kids tech usage so that there kids can create a healthy relationship with technology. I applied my UX background and design thinking skills when prototyping and engineering our iOS app on Swift. I also received hands on coaching from Microsoft product managers and developers.

PROBLEM AREA



Our probelm area was tech addiction. I see this as a growing issue within society, especially in children. Personally, I see this when kids are with family and the kids only want to play on their devices instead of interacting with others. We wanted to nip this addiction in the bud because it’s important for kids to have a healthy relationship with tech early on so that they become responsible tech users in the future.

USER IDEATION


To represent our user demographic we created two personas: a working parent and her child. This initiated empathy within my team and allowed us to better understand our user's experiences, behaviors, and goals. It also shaped our user journeys.



Next, with Wendy and Charlie as our inspiration, we created a user journey map to solidify our goals and understand the relationship between our user and app.



Lastly, we generated user stories to show key, wanted features of the app. This ensured we created an app that will be useful to our users.



COMPETITIVE ANALYSIS


To better understand the current market, we performed a SWOT analysis (Strengths, weaknesses, opportunities, and threats).


We saw that our biggest competitor was Apple Screen Time. It is successful because it has a beautiful, friendly UI, it’s already integrated into the iPhone, and can be used across multiple devices.

But I saw that there was a gap with Apple Screen Time and other tech time moderator apps. They all focused on monitoring and setting time limits on screen time and apps, but they lacked a fun, interactive aspect for kids. That’s when I had the idea of Tommi, a cute, lovable interactive avatar that would be the mediator between the parent and child. This would be a more digestible and fun way for kids to understand tech time boundaries.

APP IDEA


After a team brainstorming session, we listed the app’s main components and avatar.


USER RESEARCH BBQ


At the user research BBQ I pitched our initial app idea to parents and children. As a team, we pitched our app idea and received positive feedback from 12 parents and 2 children.


KEY TAKEAWAYS:

1. One family of 4 kids told us that they already have a system like Tommi at home, but with
physical coins so they thought it would be great to have it organized into one app

2. Some parents did not want their kids to be able to buy more app time

3. The kids responded positively to Tommi and thought he was adorable

4. Most parents said they liked the quest system the most, as this was unique and useful

IDEATION




After the user research BBQ, my team discussed the feedback. Customization was an important core value because every family is different and has different rules and values around technology.

Since some parents stated they did not want their kid to have more tech time than intially allowed. It was meaningful to our users that we create another option for the kids to spend their earned time coins on. I thought that an accessory store would be a fun way for the kids to interact with Tommi.



After talking with our product manager coach, we were unsure what kind of app statistics we wanted to show the parent. Our coach challenged us to think about how the statistics can support our goal of helping create a healthy relationship with tech. After a lot of discussion, we concluded in showing parents graphs showing their kid’s app time usage per app category and how much time coins their kid spends on each app category.

ENGINEERING


We developed our app in Swift using the XCode IDE and Git to collaborate and resolve conflicts. Our overarching goal was to create a minimal viable product (MVP) of our app consisting of the quest system, app time limitation, and Tommi accessory store.

We implemented Firebase to store each user's data. The data includes, username, password, pin code, time limit per category, time coin amount, and quests. I was responsible for developing the kid’s homepage where they can spend time coins on more app time and Tommi’s accessory store.


Kid home page on iOS app


I used a progress bar to show how much time the kid had left for each app category; I retrieved this data from firebase. There is a plus button next to the progress bar that the kid can press to increase their app time by 5 minutes, which means spending 5 time coins. After pressing the button, the progress bar will increase and the kid's time coin amount will decrease and update firebase’s coin data. Additionally, a pop up telling the kid to check their quests will appear if the kid does not have enough time coins to buy more time.


Tommi store on iOS app


I also developed the avatar store. Each avatar accessory has a price and button to buy and then equip that accessory onto Tommi.

FUTURE FEATURES


If we had more time to develop Tommi, we would allow the user to interact with Tommi through voice commands. It would also be useful to integrate machine learning to automatically adjust app time restrictions based on app usage history and give parents suggested time limits based on the kid's weekly time spent on tech.

Based on our user research, customization is important, so my idea was to implement a feature to add to the quest system where parents can create fun, redeemable activities, such as getting icecream, that the kid could save up and redeem with their time coins.

TAKEAWAYS


Explore your career path

Through this internship I was able to explore the intersection of design, computer science, and technology. I realized that I want puruse UX design in the tech field, but also know the importance of understanding the technical side of projects.



Find the sweet spot

My team worked on our own features of the app, and initially we would rarely merge our code together. This caused major merge conflicts; some resulted in nuking the code. I quickly realized I had to merge smaller portions. But the trick is finding the sweet spot between too big and too small. If it's too big conflicts occur, and if it's too small it gets tedious.
